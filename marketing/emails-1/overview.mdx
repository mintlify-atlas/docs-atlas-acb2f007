## What's inside

This hub captures Genspark chat outputs on email marketing. It covers campaign strategy, automated sequence design, and the copywriting principles that make email a high-ROI channel when applied consistently.

## Email marketing strategy

Email is the owned channel with the highest direct return for most businesses. These frameworks address how to build and deploy email as a strategic asset rather than a broadcast tool.

**Topics covered:**

- List building strategy — growing a list of people who actually want to hear from you, not just raw subscriber counts
- Segmentation — dividing your list by behavior, interest, or stage to send more relevant messages
- Send frequency — how often to email without burning out your list, and how to calibrate based on engagement data
- Deliverability fundamentals — technical and behavioral factors that determine whether your emails reach the inbox
- List health — re-engagement campaigns, sunset policies, and when to remove subscribers

## Campaign development

A campaign is a coordinated sequence of emails designed to move subscribers toward a specific action. These frameworks cover how to plan, write, and sequence campaigns.

**Topics covered:**

- Campaign types — promotional campaigns, launch sequences, content campaigns, and relationship-building campaigns
- Email structure — subject line, preview text, body, and CTA; how each element contributes to conversion
- Subject line writing — the single most important variable in whether an email gets opened
- Body copy — applying conversion copywriting principles (from the Copywriting hub) specifically to email
- CTA design — how to write and position calls to action that generate clicks without feeling pushy

## Automated sequences

Automation turns email into a scalable system. These frameworks address the most important automated sequences and how to build them.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Welcome sequence">
    The most important automation you'll build. New subscribers are at peak engagement immediately after signing up. A well-structured welcome sequence sets expectations, delivers immediate value, and begins building the relationship that determines long-term engagement.

    Typical structure: 3–5 emails over 5–7 days. Email 1 delivers the promised lead magnet and sets expectations. Emails 2–4 share your best content and story. Email 5 makes a soft offer or asks a question.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Nurture sequence">
    Designed for subscribers who are not yet ready to buy. The goal is to stay top of mind, build trust, and surface the right offer when the timing is right.

    Typical structure: Ongoing, often weekly. Mix of value content, case studies, and occasional offers. Segmentation matters here — nurture sequences should adapt based on what subscribers engage with.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Sales sequence">
    A time-boxed sequence designed to convert subscribers into customers. Typically tied to a launch, promotion, or deadline.

    Typical structure: 5–7 emails over 7–10 days. Opens with value/story, builds toward an offer, handles objections in the middle emails, creates urgency toward the close, and sends a final reminder.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Re-engagement sequence">
    For inactive subscribers. The goal is to either re-activate their interest or identify them as safe to remove from the list.

    Typical structure: 2–3 emails. First email acknowledges the gap and offers something valuable. Second email creates mild urgency. Third email is a break-up email — tell them you'll remove them if they don't engage. This last email often has the highest engagement of the three.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Email copywriting principles

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Subject lines">
    Subject lines determine open rate. The best subject lines create curiosity, promise a specific benefit, or reference something immediately relevant to the subscriber.

    Principles:
    - Keep it under 50 characters for mobile
    - Avoid spam trigger words ("free", "guaranteed", "act now")
    - Test curiosity gaps vs. direct benefit statements
    - Use the preview text as a second subject line, not a repetition of the first
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Body copy">
    Email body copy should feel personal, not broadcast. Write as if to one person, not a list.

    Principles:
    - One idea per email — don't try to cover multiple topics
    - Short paragraphs — 1–3 sentences; walls of text get skipped
    - Story before pitch — earn the right to make an offer by delivering value first
    - Active voice, conversational tone
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Calls to action">
    A CTA should be specific, low-friction, and singular. One email, one action.

    Principles:
    - Tell them exactly what to do and what they'll get ("Read the guide" not "Click here")
    - Reduce friction — the fewer steps between the email and the desired outcome, the better
    - Match the CTA to the email's energy — a soft nurture email should have a soft CTA
    - Repeat the CTA at the end of longer emails
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Tip>Subject line testing is the highest-leverage optimization in email marketing. A 5% improvement in open rate compounds across every email you send. Make subject line testing a consistent practice, not a one-time experiment.</Tip>
