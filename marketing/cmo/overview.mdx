## What's inside

This hub captures Genspark chat outputs on marketing at the executive level. It covers how to think about marketing strategy holistically — from positioning and resource allocation to building marketing teams and reporting on impact.

## Marketing strategy

CMO-level thinking starts with positioning and works outward. These frameworks address the strategic decisions that determine whether tactical execution succeeds or fails.

**Topics covered:**

- Positioning — defining what your brand stands for, who it's for, and why it wins against alternatives
- Go-to-market strategy — how to bring a product or offer to market in a way that generates early traction
- Channel selection — choosing the right marketing channels based on where your audience actually spends time and what your team can execute well
- Messaging architecture — the hierarchy of messages from core positioning down to channel-specific copy
- Competitive differentiation — identifying and communicating what makes your offer genuinely different

## Marketing leadership

Leading a marketing function requires a different skill set than executing marketing. These frameworks address the organizational and leadership dimensions of the CMO role.

**Topics covered:**

- Building a marketing team — what roles to hire first, how to structure a lean team, when to use agencies vs. in-house
- Managing marketing agencies — how to brief, evaluate, and hold agencies accountable
- Cross-functional alignment — working with sales, product, and leadership to ensure marketing efforts connect to business outcomes
- Marketing operations — the systems, tools, and processes that make a marketing team efficient
- Culture and creativity — creating conditions where marketing teams do their best work

## Planning and measurement

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Annual marketing planning">
    A CMO's annual plan sets the priorities that everything else flows from. It should connect directly to business goals, define the 3–5 big bets the marketing function will make, and establish the metrics that will prove or disprove whether those bets are working.

    Key components: Situation analysis (where are we?), goals (where do we need to go?), strategy (what are the big moves?), tactics (what will we actually do?), budget (what will it cost?), measurement (how will we know if it's working?).
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Budget allocation">
    Marketing budget decisions are high-stakes because they're hard to reverse mid-year. The frameworks here cover how to think about allocating spend across channels, between brand and performance marketing, and between existing programs and new experiments.

    Principle: Protect what's working before funding experiments. Establish a baseline allocation to proven channels, then ring-fence a percentage (typically 10–20%) for testing.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Marketing metrics and reporting">
    The metrics you report on shape what your team optimizes for. CMO-level reporting should connect marketing activity to business outcomes — revenue, pipeline, and customer acquisition — rather than stopping at channel-level metrics like impressions or followers.

    Key metrics: Customer acquisition cost (CAC), lifetime value (LTV), LTV:CAC ratio, marketing-sourced pipeline, brand awareness (if measured), and channel-level efficiency metrics as supporting data.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Quarterly reviews">
    A structured quarterly review keeps strategy connected to reality. Review what was planned vs. what was executed, what the data says about what's working, what needs to change, and what the priorities are for the next quarter.

    Format: Keep it short and decision-oriented. The goal is not to report on everything — it's to surface the decisions that need to be made.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Strategic frameworks

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Positioning" icon="crosshairs">
    Define your market category, target customer, differentiated value, and proof points. Everything in marketing flows from a clear positioning statement.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Funnel management" icon="filter">
    Map your customer journey from first awareness to purchase and advocacy. Identify the biggest drop-offs and prioritize fixing those before optimizing what's already working.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Channel strategy" icon="share-nodes">
    Don't try to be everywhere. Choose 2–3 channels where your audience is and where you can execute consistently. Depth beats breadth.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Brand vs. performance" icon="scale-balanced">
    Short-term performance marketing generates leads now. Long-term brand marketing reduces the cost of future performance marketing. Both matter; neither should be neglected entirely.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## How to use this hub

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with positioning">
    If your positioning is unclear, every other marketing decision is harder. Use the positioning frameworks to sharpen how you describe what you do and who it's for.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Audit your current channel mix">
    Apply the channel selection framework to evaluate whether your current mix is right for your audience and team. Don't add channels — eliminate the ones that aren't working.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Build your measurement framework">
    Define the 3–5 metrics that will tell you whether marketing is contributing to business goals. Report on these consistently.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Plan at the right altitude">
    Use the annual planning framework to set priorities, then let your team own execution. CMO-level thinking is most valuable at the strategy and resource-allocation layer.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>The most common CMO failure mode is over-investing in tactics before the strategy is clear. A mediocre strategy executed well outperforms a brilliant tactic in a strategic vacuum. Get positioning and channel selection right before optimizing anything else.</Tip>
