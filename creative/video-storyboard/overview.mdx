## Overview

Video Storyboard captures the planning and pre-production knowledge needed to produce structured, intentional video content. Content here covers storyboarding technique, shot list creation, visual scripting, and scene breakdown — drawn from Genspark sessions on video production planning.

## Storyboarding process

<Steps>
  <Step title="Start with the script or brief">
    Before drawing a single frame, have a complete script or creative brief. Storyboards visualize decisions that should already be made about narrative, pacing, and key moments.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Identify key scenes and beats">
    Break the script into scenes and identify the emotional or narrative beats within each scene. These beats drive frame selection — storyboard the moments that matter, not every second.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Sketch rough frames">
    Draw rough thumbnails for each key beat. Focus on composition and camera angle, not artistic polish. Stick figures and basic shapes communicate everything a storyboard needs to.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Add camera direction notes">
    Annotate each frame with camera movement (pan, tilt, dolly, static), shot type (wide, medium, close-up, ECU), and any special instructions for the camera operator or editor.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Create the shot list">
    Convert the storyboard into a numbered shot list. Group shots by location to optimize shooting schedule. Include estimated duration for each shot.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review with the team">
    Walk through the storyboard and shot list with the director, cinematographer, and editor before production begins. Identify any gaps, impractical shots, or missing coverage.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Shot types reference

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Wide shots">
    **Extreme Wide Shot (EWS):** Establishes location. Subject is tiny in the frame or absent.

    **Wide Shot (WS):** Shows the full subject and surrounding environment. Used for establishing scenes and showing spatial relationships.

    **Medium Wide Shot (MWS):** Subject visible from knees up. A common social and interview framing.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Medium shots">
    **Medium Shot (MS):** Subject from waist up. The standard conversational framing for dialogue and interviews.

    **Medium Close-Up (MCU):** Subject from chest up. Intimacy without the intensity of a close-up. Widely used in documentary and narrative work.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Close-up shots">
    **Close-Up (CU):** Face fills the frame. Conveys emotion and draws the viewer into the subject's experience.

    **Extreme Close-Up (ECU):** A single feature — eye, hand, object. Used for detail emphasis or dramatic effect.

    **Insert shot:** An ECU of a specific object or detail within a scene, used to draw attention to story-critical information.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Camera movement">
    **Static:** Camera is fixed. Conveys stability, observation, or tension.

    **Pan:** Horizontal rotation. Follows action or reveals a scene.

    **Tilt:** Vertical rotation. Reveals height or scale.

    **Dolly/Track:** Camera moves physically toward, away from, or alongside the subject. Creates a sense of depth and movement.

    **Handheld:** Intentional camera shake. Adds energy, urgency, or documentary realism.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Storyboard frame elements

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Composition" icon="crop">
    Apply the rule of thirds: place subjects and horizons along the grid lines rather than centered. Lead room — leave space in the direction a subject is looking or moving. Frame within a frame to add depth.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Continuity" icon="arrow-right-arrow-left">
    Maintain consistent screen direction across cuts. If a subject exits frame right in one shot, they should enter frame left in the next. Establish and maintain the 180° line to avoid disorienting the viewer.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Coverage" icon="shield">
    Plan enough coverage so the editor has options. For dialogue scenes, shoot the master, then individual close-ups for each speaker, then reaction shots and cutaways. Under-coverage forces the editor to work with limited material.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Transitions" icon="right-left">
    Note intended transitions in the storyboard: cut, dissolve, wipe, match cut, or jump cut. Match cuts — where shape, motion, or color matches across a cut — require planning at the storyboard stage to execute effectively.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

<Note>A storyboard does not need to be polished artwork. Its purpose is to communicate intent clearly to the production team. Clarity and completeness matter more than drawing quality.</Note>
