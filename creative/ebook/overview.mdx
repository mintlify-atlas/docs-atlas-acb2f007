## Writing process

<Steps>
  <Step title="Define your reader and outcome">
    Identify the specific reader this eBook serves and the single outcome they should achieve after reading it. A well-defined reader and outcome constrain scope and prevent topic sprawl.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Build a detailed outline">
    Write a chapter-by-chapter outline with section headings and one-sentence summaries for each section. The outline is your contract with the reader — it should be comprehensive enough to guide drafting without restricting discovery.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Draft by section, not chapter">
    Work through the outline one section at a time. Complete drafts are better than polished fragments. Resist editing while drafting.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Structural edit first">
    Before line editing, review the structure. Check that each chapter fulfills its purpose, transitions are logical, and the argument builds correctly to the conclusion.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Line edit and proofread">
    Edit for clarity, sentence variety, and voice consistency. Proofread last, after all structural changes are final.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Design and format">
    Apply layout, typography, and visual elements. Export to target formats and verify formatting in each output.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Publish and distribute">
    Upload to distribution platforms. Configure metadata, pricing, and access. Prepare a launch sequence.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Format reference

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="PDF">
    **Best for:** Direct downloads, lead magnets, gated content.

    PDF preserves exact layout and is universally readable. Use for designed eBooks where visual formatting is important. Not ideal for variable screen sizes or reflowable reading.

    **Tools:** Adobe InDesign, Affinity Publisher, Canva (for simpler layouts).
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="ePub">
    **Best for:** E-reader distribution (Kindle, Kobo, Apple Books), accessibility.

    ePub is a reflowable format that adapts to screen size and reader font preferences. Required for most e-reader platforms. Supports accessibility features including screen readers.

    **Tools:** Vellum, Sigil, Scrivener (export), Calibre.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Web/HTML">
    **Best for:** Gated content on a website, interactive eBooks.

    HTML-based eBooks can include interactive elements, embedded media, and analytics. Suited to content that benefits from linking and interactivity.

    **Tools:** Any web authoring tool or static site generator.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Cover design principles

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Hierarchy and title legibility" icon="font">
    The title must be legible at thumbnail size (the size at which most readers encounter it online). Use large, high-contrast type. Subtitle and author name take secondary visual weight.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Category conventions" icon="book">
    Cover design signals genre and category. Business eBooks tend toward clean, minimal layouts with sans-serif type. Study successful covers in your category before designing yours.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Color and contrast" icon="palette">
    Use a limited palette of 2-3 colors. Ensure strong contrast between background and text. A bold, distinctive color makes the cover recognizable in a list of search results.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Resolution and formats" icon="image">
    Design at 2560 × 1600 px minimum for high-resolution screens. Export in both RGB (screen) and CMYK (print) if producing a print version alongside the digital edition.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
