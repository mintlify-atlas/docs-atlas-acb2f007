## Overview

Tone & Voice documents how to communicate consistently across every channel and format. Content here covers brand personality, messaging hierarchy, audience-specific framing, and the practical rules that keep writing on-brand.

<Tip>Consistent tone builds trust over time. Even small deviations — a single overly formal email or a flippant social post — erode the coherence of a brand voice.</Tip>

## Voice vs. tone

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Voice">
    Voice is the brand's consistent personality — the traits that remain stable regardless of what is being communicated or who is reading it. Think of voice as the character of the brand.

    **Example traits:** Confident, clear, human, grounded.

    Voice does not change between a product announcement and a support article. The same character shows up in both.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Tone">
    Tone is the emotional inflection applied in a specific context. It adapts to the situation, the audience, and the content type.

    **Example tones:**
    - Celebratory in a launch post
    - Calm and reassuring in an error message
    - Direct and efficient in technical documentation

    Tone variation is how a brand feels human — it responds to context the way a person does.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Messaging framework

<Steps>
  <Step title="Define the core message">
    Start with the single most important thing you want the audience to take away. Every piece of communication should be traceable back to this core message.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Identify audience segments">
    Define who you are talking to. Different segments have different pain points, vocabulary, and levels of familiarity with your product or brand.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Map messages to segments">
    Adapt the core message for each segment. The underlying truth stays the same; the framing, examples, and emphasis shift to match what each audience cares about.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Choose the right channel">
    Match the message to the format best suited for delivery — email for depth, social for reach, in-product for action, video for demonstration.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Test and iterate">
    Review communication outputs against the voice guidelines. Identify patterns that drift from the brand and update the guidelines to capture new edge cases.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Do and don't

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Word choice" icon="spell-check">
    **Do:** Use plain language. Prefer short words over long ones when meaning is equivalent. Write "use" not "utilize", "help" not "facilitate".

    **Don't:** Use jargon to sound authoritative. Jargon excludes readers who are unfamiliar with it and makes copy feel cold.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Sentence length" icon="ruler-horizontal">
    **Do:** Vary sentence length. Short sentences create emphasis. Longer sentences can carry more nuance when structured clearly.

    **Don't:** Default to long, complex sentences. Each sentence should carry one idea. If a sentence requires re-reading, break it into two.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Active vs. passive voice" icon="arrow-right">
    **Do:** Write in active voice. "We shipped the update" is clearer and more direct than "The update was shipped".

    **Don't:** Overuse passive voice to avoid accountability. It makes copy feel evasive and bureaucratic.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Contractions" icon="quote-left">
    **Do:** Use contractions in conversational contexts ("you'll", "we've", "it's"). They make copy feel more human.

    **Don't:** Use contractions in formal legal, compliance, or high-stakes error contexts where precision and formality build trust.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
