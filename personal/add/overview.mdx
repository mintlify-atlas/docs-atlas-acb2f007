## Key topics

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="ADHD and executive function">
    ADHD is fundamentally a disorder of executive function — the set of mental skills that includes working memory, flexible thinking, and self-control. Research covers how these deficits manifest in real life and why standard productivity advice often fails for people with ADHD.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Medication overview">
    Stimulant medications (amphetamines, methylphenidate) are the most effective pharmacological treatment for ADHD. Non-stimulant options (atomoxetine, guanfacine, bupropion) are used when stimulants are not tolerated or appropriate. Research covers how these medications work, what to expect, and how to work with a prescriber.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Behavioral and cognitive strategies">
    Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) adapted for ADHD, ADHD coaching, and behavioral interventions show meaningful benefits alongside or without medication. Research covers practical techniques for improving organization, follow-through, and emotional regulation.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Environmental design">
    The environment can compensate for executive function deficits. Research covers how to reduce friction in routines, use visual cues and external reminders, structure workspaces to minimize distraction, and build systems that require less willpower.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Sleep, exercise, and ADHD">
    Sleep problems are common in ADHD and worsen symptoms. Exercise has well-documented benefits for attention and impulse control. Research covers how to prioritize these foundations and why they are particularly important for people with ADHD.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="ADHD in adults">
    Adult ADHD is often under-diagnosed and presents differently than childhood ADHD. Research covers how symptoms shift with age, how late diagnosis affects identity, and strategies for managing ADHD in professional and relationship contexts.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Practical strategies

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="Task management">
    External systems do the work your working memory cannot. Use a trusted task manager, write everything down immediately, break tasks into the smallest possible next action, and use time-boxing to limit open-ended work sessions. Body doubling — working alongside another person — is a simple technique that improves focus for many people with ADHD.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Time awareness">
    ADHD is associated with "time blindness" — difficulty sensing the passage of time. Strategies include using visible timers, setting alarms for transitions, and building time buffers into your schedule. The Pomodoro technique works well for some; time blocking works better for others.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Emotional regulation">
    Rejection sensitive dysphoria (RSD) and emotional reactivity are common in ADHD. Strategies include naming emotions as they arise, building in recovery time after stressful interactions, and working with a therapist familiar with ADHD.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="Routine building">
    Routines reduce the cognitive load of daily decisions. Start small, attach new habits to existing anchors (habit stacking), and use checklists for recurring sequences. Expect that routines will break and plan in advance for how to restart them.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

<Note>ADHD diagnosis and medication management require a qualified clinician. This knowledge base surfaces research and strategies — it does not substitute for professional evaluation or treatment.</Note>
