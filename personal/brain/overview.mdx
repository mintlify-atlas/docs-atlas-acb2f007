## Key topics

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Sleep and the brain">
    Sleep is the single most important factor in cognitive performance and long-term brain health. During sleep, the glymphatic system clears metabolic waste including amyloid beta — a protein associated with Alzheimer's disease. Research covers sleep architecture, how to improve sleep quality, and the cognitive consequences of sleep deprivation.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Exercise and neurogenesis">
    Aerobic exercise stimulates BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor), which supports the growth of new neurons and synaptic connections. Research covers how much exercise is needed, which types are most beneficial, and how physical activity interacts with cognitive aging.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Nutrition for the brain">
    The brain is metabolically demanding and sensitive to nutritional quality. Research covers the evidence for omega-3 fatty acids, antioxidants, Mediterranean-style diets, and how blood sugar regulation affects cognition.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Stress and cortisol">
    Chronic stress elevates cortisol, which damages the hippocampus — a brain region critical for memory. Research covers how stress affects cognitive function, the evidence for stress management practices (mindfulness, breathing exercises, social connection), and how to build stress resilience.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Cognitive training and learning">
    Spaced repetition, active recall, interleaving, and deliberate practice are among the most evidence-supported learning strategies. Research covers how these techniques work, how to implement them, and the limits of commercial "brain training" apps.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Supplements and nootropics">
    Research covers the evidence (and lack thereof) for common nootropic supplements including omega-3s, creatine, lion's mane mushroom, bacopa monnieri, and caffeine. Separating marketing claims from clinical evidence is a recurring theme.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## Brain health pillars

<Steps>
  <Step title="Protect sleep">
    Prioritize 7–9 hours of quality sleep. Consistent sleep and wake times, a cool dark room, and limiting light exposure in the evening are the highest-leverage interventions.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Move every day">
    Even moderate aerobic exercise several times per week produces meaningful cognitive benefits. Walking, cycling, swimming, and zone 2 cardio are all effective.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Manage chronic stress">
    Build daily stress management practices — whether mindfulness, time in nature, social connection, or creative activity. Chronic, unmanaged stress is directly damaging to brain structure over time.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Eat for brain health">
    Prioritize whole foods, omega-3 rich sources (fatty fish, walnuts, flaxseed), leafy greens, and berries. Limit ultra-processed foods and excessive sugar.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Keep learning">
    Cognitive reserve — built through education, learning new skills, and social engagement — is protective against age-related cognitive decline. Stay curious.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Tip>The lifestyle factors that are best for cardiovascular health — exercise, diet, sleep, stress management — are also the most evidence-supported interventions for brain health. There is no brain health stack more effective than these fundamentals.</Tip>
