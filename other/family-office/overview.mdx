## Key concepts

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Single-family office (SFO)">
    A private entity set up to manage the wealth and affairs of one wealthy family. SFOs offer maximum control and customization but require significant assets to justify the operational overhead. They typically handle investments, tax planning, estate administration, philanthropy, and family services.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Multi-family office (MFO)">
    A shared family office structure serving multiple families. MFOs provide many of the same services as an SFO but distribute costs across clients, making professional family office services accessible at lower asset thresholds.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Family governance frameworks">
    Formal structures that define how a family makes decisions about shared wealth, business interests, and philanthropic activities. Governance documents may include a family constitution, investment policy statement, and family employment policy.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Succession planning">
    The process of preparing for the transfer of leadership and ownership in a family business or wealth structure. Effective succession planning addresses both the technical (legal, tax) and human (relationships, communication) dimensions.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Trust structures">
    Legal arrangements used to hold and manage assets for beneficiaries. Common structures in family office contexts include revocable living trusts, irrevocable trusts, dynasty trusts, and charitable remainder trusts.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
